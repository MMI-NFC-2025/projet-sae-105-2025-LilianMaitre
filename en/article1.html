Vera Rubin, pioneer of astronomy and explorer of the Universe’s mysteries

Born on 23 July 1928 in Philadelphia, Vera Cooper Rubin developed a passion for the stars at the age of twelve, encouraged by her father, who helped her build her first telescope. In a scientific world dominated by men, she still chose to study astronomy.

She completed her studies at Vassar College, one of the rare institutions that accepted women in astronomy at the time, then defended a thesis on the distribution of galaxies under the supervision of physicist George Gamow. From the very start of her career, her sense of observation and ambition for fundamental research stood out.

Throughout her life, Vera Rubin often found herself as the only woman in research teams and had to juggle her work with family life, raising four children while pursuing her scientific projects.

Pioneering studies in the face of adversity

From the 1960s onwards, Rubin carried out major observing campaigns at Kitt Peak and then at Mount Palomar Observatory, which was still largely closed to women. By measuring the rotation speed of stars in the outer regions of galaxies, she noticed that they moved just as fast as those nearer the centre.

This result contradicted classical laws and revealed the presence of an invisible mass now known as dark matter, whose gravity holds galaxies together. The discovery was revolutionary and turned dark matter into a central topic in modern cosmology.

The impact of the Matilda effect on her career

Despite the strength of her results and their worldwide impact, Vera Rubin never received the Nobel Prize and did not benefit from the same honours as her male colleagues. She has therefore become an emblematic example of the Matilda effect, the erasure of women from scientific history.

Beyond her discoveries, Rubin left a deep mark through her humility and generosity. She cared more about the progress of science than about awards and often said that the greatest compliment was that her data continued to be used by other researchers.

As a mentor, she strongly supported young women who wanted to become astronomers, writing recommendation letters herself and following their careers closely. Astronomer Sandra Moore Faber later described her as “a guiding light for a whole generation of women astronomers”.

A legacy engraved in science

Recognition did eventually come, with the National Medal of Science in 1993 and the Gold Medal of the Royal Astronomical Society in 1996. Today one of the world’s largest observatory projects bears her name: the Vera C. Rubin Observatory in Chile, designed to revolutionise our view of the sky.

Rubin passed away in 2016, leaving behind a more open scientific community and a better‑understood Universe. Her legacy continues to inspire anyone interested in science, equity and curiosity.
