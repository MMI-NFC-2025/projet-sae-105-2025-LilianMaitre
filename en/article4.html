Interstellar

The film “Interstellar” puts female scientists in the spotlight through complex, powerful characters. This article looks at the impact of this representation on the image of women in science, its limitations and its educational implications.

Women scientists at the heart of the scenario

In Christopher Nolan’s “Interstellar”, the rehabilitation of female scientists is embodied mainly through Murphy Cooper and Amelia Brand. These characters give new visibility to women scientists on screen, in a cinematic universe where they are still too often relegated to secondary or stereotypical roles.

Murphy Cooper

Murphy, played by Jessica Chastain as an adult, is a brilliant physicist at NASA. She is not just the “hero’s daughter” but plays a crucial role in advancing the plot: thanks to her scientific determination and skills in mathematics and theoretical physics, she manages to solve the equation that will save humanity.

Surrounded by equations and research work, Murphy is shown as a real agent of scientific progress, far from the clichés of the muse or assistant. Her perseverance in the face of doubt, lost years and late recognition subtly echoes the careers of many real‑life women scientists, including Vera Rubin.

Amelia Brand

Amelia Brand, played by Anne Hathaway, leads the main space mission. She embodies scientific determination, courage, leadership and a strong capacity for taking risks.

However, the script often places the emphasis on her emotions and affective choices, sometimes at the expense of scientific decisions. This recalls certain persistent stereotypes in how female characters are written, even though Amelia remains a competent scientist, committed to survival and progress.

Impact and limitations of representation in Interstellar

“Interstellar” allows a very broad audience to identify with intelligent, passionate female characters who play decisive roles in solving complex scientific problems. The film helps break down the image of the exclusively male scientist, offers contemporary role models and can encourage young girls to consider scientific careers.

Scenes in which Murphy works beneath blackboards filled with equations or in NASA laboratories contribute to a positive, empowering representation of women scientists. At the same time, they invite viewers to reflect on the obstacles that still exist, both on screen and in reality.
