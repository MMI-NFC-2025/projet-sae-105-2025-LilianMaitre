Women of
science
in literature

Long relegated to the shadows of scholarly accounts, women scientists are now becoming heroines of children’s and popular literature. This article explores how publishing is re‑evaluating inspiring figures and contributing to more diverse role models.

Invisibility and revaluation: what are the trends?

The representation of women in science in literature still vacillates between invisibility and recognition. For a long time, female scientists were confined to supporting roles, but in recent years they have increasingly become central characters, driven by renewed interest from publishers and educators.

Marie Curie remains a key reference yet often hides the “forest” of other pioneers. Many books still focus mainly on her, sometimes at the expense of other figures who are just as essential for building diverse role models. However, contemporary works for young readers and the general public now highlight scientists such as Rosalind Franklin, Ada Lovelace, Émilie du Châtelet, Katherine Johnson, Maryam Mirzakhani and Vera Rubin.

When young people reimagine the pioneers

Dictionaries, anthologies and children’s books bring together short biographies, stories of discoveries and illustrations that show the passion, challenges and achievements of these women. They describe how their vocation began, how they had to fight for a place in the scientific world, the obstacles they encountered (sexism, isolation, lack of recognition) and the impact of their work.

Children’s literature often uses documentary novels or illustrated stories to humanise these paths and inspire new generations, showing that science is also a female adventure. Fictional or real interviews, timelines and galleries highlight a wide range of disciplines: astronomy, biology, chemistry, mathematics, computer science and more.

Imagination and science fiction: thinking differently about women’s access

Novels and science‑fiction stories also offer powerful ways to reflect on women’s access to science, whether through speculative futures or more rebellious narratives. By projecting heroines into laboratories, observatories or space missions, these stories question stereotypes and explore alternative possibilities.

Little by little, literature is making visible the richness and diversity of women in science. It challenges historical exclusions and invites readers to reinterpret the scientific narrative through the lens of equality, emancipation and inspiration.

One example is the book Femmes de science : la rencontre de 14 chercheuses d’hier et d’aujourd’hui (in French), which presents fourteen women scientists through accessible portraits and illustrations.
