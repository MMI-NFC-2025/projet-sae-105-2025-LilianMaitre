Popularisation of
Vera Rubin's theory
and further study

How did Vera Rubin revolutionise cosmology? Through her study of galaxy rotation, discover dark matter, a central enigma of the modern Universe, still at the heart of major research in astrophysics.

Classical laws and galactic rotation*

In the 1970s, Vera Rubin focused on how stars revolve around the centre of galaxies, particularly spiral galaxies such as Andromeda. According to classical laws such as Newton’s, stars farther from the centre should rotate more slowly, like planets in the Solar System.

However, Rubin and Kent Ford observed a surprising anomaly: stars at the outskirts of galaxies rotate just as fast as those near the centre. The resulting rotation curves are “flat”, while theoretical models predicted a sharp drop with distance.

The existence of invisible matter

To explain this phenomenon, Rubin proposed the existence of a massive but invisible component: dark matter. It cannot be seen directly because it neither emits nor absorbs light, but it exerts a gravitational pull on stars and gas in galaxies.

This implies a huge “missing” mass acting like a gravitational glue that prevents galaxies from flying apart under their rotation. Current estimates suggest that dark matter makes up more than 80% of the total mass in the observable Universe.

Current challenges in dark matter research

Today, dark matter is a central topic in astrophysics. It has still not been detected directly: its presence is inferred from its effects, but its exact nature remains unknown. Researchers are exploring several hypotheses, including new massive particles that interact only very weakly with light and ordinary matter.

Thanks to Vera Rubin’s work, astronomers realised that a large part of the Universe escapes our direct perception. Her research paved the way for modern cosmology, changing our view of the cosmos and launching one of the greatest scientific quests of our time.
