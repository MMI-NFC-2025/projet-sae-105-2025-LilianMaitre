Matilda timeline

Unfortunately, Vera Rubin was not the only woman to face this kind of injustice; many others have been forgotten or erased from scientific history. This timeline retraces the journeys and major discoveries of great “Matildas” whose work has revolutionised science, often without the recognition they deserved.

Scroll through the decades to see how the Matilda effect highlights the struggle for visibility, diversity and equality in science, and to honour the women who changed our understanding of the world.

1815

Ada Lovelace

Created the first algorithm for a machine and is often considered the first computer programmer, yet long reduced to the role of assistant.

1897

Lise Meitner

Co‑discoverer of nuclear fission. The Nobel Prize was awarded only to Otto Hahn, erasing her decisive contribution.

1958

Rosalind Franklin

Produced key X‑ray images that revealed the structure of DNA, but recognition went mainly to Watson and Crick.

1967

Jocelyn Bell Burnell

Discovered pulsars as a PhD student, yet the Nobel Prize was awarded to her thesis supervisor.

1970

Vera Rubin

Demonstrated the presence of dark matter through galaxy rotation curves, but was never recognised by the Nobel Prize.
